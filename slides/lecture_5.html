Features of Bowen’s Reaction Series

  • Overview
    • Bowen’s Reaction Series describes the sequence of mineral crystallization from a cooling magma.
    • It helps explain the mineral composition of igneous rocks and their relationship to magma evolution.
  • Discontinuous Branch
    • High-Temperature Minerals:
      • Olivine (First to crystallize, ~1200°C)
      • Pyroxene
      • Amphibole
      • Biotite Mica (Last to crystallize in this branch, ~750°C)
    • Mineral Changes: As temperature decreases, minerals become more complex in structure and composition.
  • Continuous Branch
    • Plagioclase Feldspar:
      • Crystallizes continuously from high to low temperature.
      • High-Temperature: Calcium-rich (Anorthite)
      • Low-Temperature: Sodium-rich (Albite)
  • Final Crystallization
    • At Lower Temperatures:
      • Muscovite Mica
      • Potassium Feldspar (Orthoclase)
      • Quartz (Last to crystallize, ~650°C)
    • Importance: These minerals are stable at the lowest temperatures and often found in felsic igneous rocks.
  • Significance
    • The series helps predict the mineral content of igneous rocks based on their cooling history.
    • Example: A basalt (mafic) typically contains olivine, pyroxene and Ca-plagiocalse, while a granite (felsic) contains quartz and feldspar.
